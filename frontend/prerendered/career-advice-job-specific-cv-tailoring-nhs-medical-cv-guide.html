NHS CV Guide: Format, Structure, and What Recruiters Expect in 2026

How to format an NHS CV: length, section order, GMC/NMC registration, clinical experience, audits, teaching, and tailoring to the band or grade in the person specification.

Updated July 11, 2026

An NHS CV leads with eligibility: professional registration (GMC or NMC), current grade or band, and required certifications near the top, followed by clinical experience in reverse-chronological order, then audit and quality improvement, teaching, and memberships. Keep it factual and easy to scan — NHS recruiters shortlist against the person specification, so every section should make an essential criterion easy to find.

This is the NHS overview page: format, structure, and what recruiters expect. For a ready-made skeleton, download the NHS CV template; for a worked example of a doctor's version, see the NHS doctor CV example.

NHS CV format and length

The format rules are conservative: a single column, standard headings, plain text, and consistent dates. Many NHS applications also pass through recruitment systems and portal parsers, so the same discipline that helps a human shortlister helps the software.

SectionWhat to include
Profile2–3 lines on grade or band, specialty interest, and NHS or equivalent experience
Registration and coursesGMC or NMC status, ALS/BLS, specialty courses, mandatory training where relevant
Clinical experienceRecent posts and rotations, patient groups, settings, responsibilities
Audit and QITopic, your role, the standard measured, the outcome, any presentation
Teaching and leadershipWho you taught, the format, feedback, rota or supervision responsibilities

On length: a Band 5–6 nursing or AHP CV is usually fine at two pages. Doctors' CVs legitimately run longer because training posts, audits, teaching, and publications each need their own section — three or more pages is normal and not penalized. What gets penalized is burying eligibility: if a shortlister cannot find your registration status in the first half page, the length was never the problem.

Registration and professional details (GMC and NMC)

  • State your registration body and status plainly near the top: GMC registration with a licence to practise for doctors, NMC registration (and part) for nurses and midwives.
  • Include your registration number if the employer asks for it; otherwise “GMC registered, licence to practise” with the year is enough at CV stage.
  • List required certifications with their official names and expiry-relevant dates — ALS, BLS, safeguarding levels, mandatory training where the advert names it.
  • Add right-to-work or visa status when the advert requests it, and your notice period or availability where relevant.
  • Keep revalidation unremarkable: up-to-date registration is the point; only mention revalidation detail if the post specifically involves it.

Clinical experience recruiters can map to the person specification

List posts in reverse-chronological order, each with grade or band, specialty, trust or hospital, and dates. Under each post, write bullets a shortlister can match directly to the person specification: patient groups, clinical settings, procedures, and the responsibilities you actually held.

NHS posts also look for evidence beyond clinical skill: documentation, escalation, multidisciplinary working, governance, and patient safety. Bullets like these carry that evidence:

  • Documented ward reviews, discharge summaries, and escalation plans under local policy.
  • Worked with nursing, pharmacy, therapy, and senior medical colleagues during ward rounds.
  • Presented audit findings at departmental teaching and agreed a change with the supervising consultant.

Audits and quality improvement

Do not just list audit titles. For each audit or QI project, say what problem you measured, what standard you audited against, what changed as a result, and whether you re-audited. One completed audit cycle described properly is worth more than five titles — it shows you understand governance rather than just participation.

If your involvement was partial, say so accurately: “collected and analyzed data for…” is credible; implying you led something you did not is the kind of claim that unravels at interview.

Teaching and training

Recruiters want the specifics: who you taught (students, foundation doctors, healthcare assistants, patients), in what format (bedside, small group, lectures, simulation), how often, and any feedback or outcomes. Include supervision and mentoring — supporting a student nurse through a placement or supervising a junior colleague's procedures is teaching evidence, not filler.

Supporting statement basics

Many NHS applications go through NHS Jobs or Trac, where the supporting statement (supporting information) does the heavy lifting and the CV is uploaded alongside or used to populate the form. The two work together: the CV is your structured evidence bank; the supporting statement walks the person specification criterion by criterion, pointing at that evidence. Write the CV first — a well-evidenced CV makes the statement a matter of arranging, not inventing.

Tailoring your NHS CV to the band and role

Emphasis shifts with the post. For a Band 5 post, lead with core clinical competence, patient safety, and reliability under supervision. For Band 6 and above, bring coordination, mentoring, and audit involvement forward. For doctors, a trust-grade medicine post wants acute experience and ALS high on page one, while a teaching-heavy post wants your teaching section earlier. The person specification tells you exactly which — the process in how to tailor your CV to a specific job works unchanged with a person specification as the source.

If your NHS experience is limited

Do not hide non-NHS experience — translate it. Show patient safety, escalation, documentation, team communication, and governance using plain UK terminology, and map your registration progress honestly. Recruiters understand that candidates arrive through different routes; what helps is a CV that makes your current readiness clear instead of overstating it. The wider medical CV guide covers moving between UK and US conventions, and the NHS nurse CV checklist keeps nursing applications on track.

Before sending, run a final checklist pass for dates, registration details, links, and formatting — small errors read as carelessness in a safety-critical field.

Common NHS CV mistakes

  • Registration buried on page two — GMC or NMC status is an eligibility check; it belongs in the first half page.
  • Audit titles without cycles — a title proves attendance; measured-changed-re-audited proves governance.
  • Skills claimed without settings — “excellent communicator” says nothing; “handover to night team and escalation calls to the registrar” does.
  • One CV for every post — shortlisting is scored per person specification, so the same document cannot lead with the right evidence twice.
  • Inconsistent dates between the CV and the application form — mismatches trigger exactly the scrutiny you do not want in a regulated field.

Frequently asked questions

How long should an NHS CV be?

Two pages is a sensible target for most nursing and AHP posts at Band 5–6. Doctors' CVs run longer — commonly three or more pages — because registration, posts, audit, teaching, and publications each earn a section. Length is fine as long as eligibility is findable in the first half page.

Do I need a CV if the application is a form on NHS Jobs or Trac?

Usually yes, in practice. Many posts (especially medical ones) ask for an uploaded CV, and even where the form is everything, writing the CV first gives you the evidence bank your supporting statement draws from. It is also what you hand over when a recruiter or consultant asks for it directly.

What is a person specification?

The document listing the essential and desirable criteria for the post — qualifications, experience, skills, and values. NHS shortlisting is scored against it, so the strongest NHS CV is one where each essential criterion has an obvious matching line of evidence.
