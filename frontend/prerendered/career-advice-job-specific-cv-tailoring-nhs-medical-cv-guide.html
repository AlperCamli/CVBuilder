NHS Medical CV Guide: What Recruiters Expect to See

A practical NHS medical CV guide covering registration, clinical posts, audits, teaching, courses, and how to tailor evidence for UK roles.

Updated June 30, 2026

An NHS medical CV should make eligibility and clinical fit obvious. Recruiters should not have to search for your GMC status, grade, recent posts, or evidence that you understand UK clinical practice.

Think of this as the NHS overview. A broader medical CV guide helps when you are moving between UK and US formats, while the NHS nurse CV checklist keeps nursing applications from being forced into doctor-focused sections.

Start with the essentials

  • GMC registration status and license to practise.
  • Current grade, specialty, and employment setting.
  • Right to work or visa information if requested.
  • ALS or other required certifications.
  • Availability, location preferences, or notice period when relevant.

Use a recruiter-friendly order

SectionWhat to include
Profile2-3 lines on grade, specialty interest, NHS or equivalent experience
Registration and coursesGMC, ALS, specialty courses, mandatory training if relevant
Clinical experienceRecent posts, rotations, responsibilities, patient groups, supervision level
Audit and QITopic, your role, measurement, outcome, presentation
Teaching and leadershipWho you taught, format, feedback, rota or supervision responsibilities

Describe NHS fit, not just medical skill

Clinical skill matters, but NHS posts also look for documentation, escalation, multidisciplinary working, governance, patient safety, and communication. Add evidence for those areas when the job description asks for them.

  • Documented ward reviews, discharge summaries, and escalation plans under local policy.
  • Worked with nursing, pharmacy, therapy, and senior medical colleagues during ward rounds.
  • Presented audit findings at departmental teaching and agreed a change with the supervising consultant.

Keep audit and teaching specific

Do not just list audit titles. Say what problem you measured, what standard you used, what you changed, and whether you re-audited. For teaching, mention the audience and format, such as bedside teaching, simulation, induction, or exam preparation.

If your NHS experience is limited

Do not hide non-NHS experience. Translate it carefully. Show patient safety, escalation, documentation, team communication, and governance using plain wording. If you have observer, clinical attachment, or trust induction experience, label it accurately and explain what you learned about UK practice.

Recruiters understand that candidates arrive through different routes. What helps is a CV that makes your current readiness clear instead of overstating familiarity with the NHS.

After the NHS evidence is in the right order, a final checklist pass for dates, registration details, links, and formatting can catch small errors that distract from strong clinical experience.
