What Is an ATS Resume? A Plain-English Guide

Learn what an ATS resume is, how applicant tracking systems read applications, and how to keep your CV readable for software and people.

Updated June 30, 2026

An ATS resume is a resume that an applicant tracking system can read without losing important details. It is not a special secret format. It is a normal, well-structured document with clear headings, real text, and relevant wording.

ATS software helps employers collect applications, store candidate information, and search or filter submissions. A recruiter may still read your CV, so the goal is not to write for software only. The goal is to avoid formatting and wording that hides your fit.

What ATS software usually reads

  • Contact details.
  • Section headings.
  • Job titles and employers.
  • Dates.
  • Skills, tools, qualifications, and keywords.
  • Education, certifications, and licenses.

What causes problems

  • Important text placed only inside images.
  • Complex tables, columns, text boxes, or graphics.
  • Creative section names that make parsing harder.
  • Keyword stuffing that makes the CV unreadable.
  • Missing exact terms from the job description when they are true for you.

ATS-friendly does not mean boring

A clean CV can still have strong writing. The best ATS-friendly resumes use simple structure and specific evidence. They do not rely on decoration to look good, and they do not repeat the same keyword ten times.

Be careful with ATS score myths

Some tools sell a single ATS score, but employers use different systems and settings. A score can be a useful prompt, not a guarantee. The better test is whether the CV uses the employer's important terms naturally, keeps facts easy to parse, and remains readable to a person.
