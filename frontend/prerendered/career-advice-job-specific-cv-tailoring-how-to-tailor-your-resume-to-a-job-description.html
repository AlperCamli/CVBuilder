How to Tailor Your CV to a Job Description Without Overdoing It

A practical way to compare your CV with a job description, choose the right evidence, and make the final version feel specific but honest.

Updated June 30, 2026

Match the job description with your real experience.

Paste the role, review suggested themes, and approve only the edits that are true for your background.

Tailor your CV

Tailoring a CV does not mean rewriting your whole career for every vacancy. It means changing the emphasis so the reader can see the match faster.

The common mistake is copying job-ad language into the CV without evidence. That can sound optimized, but it does not feel trustworthy. A tailored CV should still sound like you.

A repeatable customization process keeps this from becoming a rewrite every time. The before-and-after resume examples make the difference easier to see: the facts stay true, but the emphasis gets sharper.

Read the job description in three passes

  • First pass: highlight the must-have requirements, such as tools, qualifications, years of experience, or clinical registration.
  • Second pass: highlight the work style, such as stakeholder management, fast-paced clinics, audit, leadership, or documentation.
  • Third pass: ignore generic words unless you can prove them with a specific example.

Build a small matching table

Job asks forYour evidenceWhere to show it
Stakeholder managementWeekly status calls with client operations and finance teamsSummary and top experience bullets
Excel or reportingBuilt a monthly dashboard used by department leadsSkills and one result-focused bullet
NHS documentationCompleted patient notes and escalation records under local protocolClinical experience section

If a requirement has no honest match, do not force it. You can still apply if the role is a stretch, but pretending creates a weak interview later.

Change these parts first

  • Summary: mention the role type and strongest matching evidence.
  • Skills: keep only skills you can defend with examples.
  • First three bullets: move the most relevant achievements up.
  • Job titles and headings: keep them accurate, but clarify context where needed.

What not to change

Do not change facts to match the advert. Dates, job titles, qualifications, registration status, and employer names should stay accurate. Tailoring is about selection and clarity. If the employer wants Kubernetes and you have only used Docker, say Docker. If they ask for ICU and your experience is acute medicine, show acute medicine honestly.

Resume keywords belong inside real evidence. A keyword in the skills section is weaker than the same idea shown inside a truthful work example.

How to know the CV is tailored enough

Read the first half of page one without looking at the job ad. A recruiter should be able to tell what role you are applying for and why your background is relevant. If the answer could still be any job in your field, tailor the summary and top bullets again.
