CV vs Resume: What to Send and What Actually Matters

Understand the difference between a CV and a resume, when each term is used, and how to choose the right format for a job application.

Updated June 30, 2026

Not sure which version to send?

Start with your full CV, then create a focused version for the job description.

Create a tailored version

The CV vs resume question feels bigger than it is because the words change by country, profession, and employer. The label matters, but it is not a quality signal. The stronger document is the one that gives the reader the right evidence quickly.

Make the first page useful enough that the reader wants to keep going.

For most applications, the naming question is less important than the evidence. A broad, unfinished document needs clearer resume writing before local terminology will make much difference.

The short difference

TermCommon useBest approach
ResumeUsually used in the US and Canada for most non-academic jobs.Keep it focused, often one or two pages.
CVCommon in the UK, Europe, healthcare, academia, and research.Match local expectations; academic CVs can be much longer.
Application CVWhat many UK and international employers mean by CV.Treat it like a focused resume, not a complete career archive.

When to use a resume

Use a resume for most private-sector roles in the US or Canada. Keep it concise, job-specific, and easy to scan. Two clear pages are better than one crowded page, but every section should earn its place.

When to use a CV

Use a CV when the employer asks for one, especially in the UK, Europe, healthcare, education, academia, and research settings. Extra detail is not automatically padding. For NHS and medical roles, the reader may expect registration, clinical experience, audits, teaching, publications, and courses.

Medical applications are the obvious exception to the short-resume instinct. The medical CV guide and NHS medical CV guide show which extra sections help the reader judge your fit.

What matters more than the label

Length is only a clue. A one-page resume can still be weak if it is vague, and a three-page CV can be useful if the role genuinely needs clinical rotations, publications, audits, or research detail. Let the employer, country, and profession decide how much detail belongs in the file, then edit for relevance within those limits.

  • Use the same wording as the job ad when it is accurate for your experience.
  • Put recent, relevant evidence before older detail.
  • Keep formatting simple enough for recruiters and ATS software.
  • Avoid sending one generic document to every employer.

A simple decision rule

Let the document name come from the closest source. If the application portal, recruiter, or job ad gives a name, use that name. If it does not, use the local convention: resume for most US private-sector roles, CV for UK and many international applications. Then move on.

The useful work starts once the label is settled. The same CV becomes a stronger application when the summary, skills, and first bullets are matched to the job description instead of left as a general career summary.
