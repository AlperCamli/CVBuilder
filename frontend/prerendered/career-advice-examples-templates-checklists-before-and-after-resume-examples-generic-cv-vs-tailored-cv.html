Before and After Resume Examples: Generic CV vs Tailored CV

See practical before-and-after resume examples that show how a generic CV becomes a tailored CV for specific job applications.

The strongest tailored CVs do not invent experience. They reorder, clarify, and rewrite existing evidence so the employer can immediately see why the candidate matches the job description.

Use these examples to learn what changes when you tailor a CV: the summary becomes sharper, the skills section reflects the role, and bullet points prove the requirements instead of listing duties.

What changes in a tailored CV?

A tailored CV usually changes five things: positioning, keyword focus, section order, bullet point evidence, and irrelevant detail.

  • Positioning: the summary names the type of role and strongest matching evidence.
  • Keyword focus: relevant terms from the job description appear naturally in the right sections.
  • Section order: the most relevant skills and experience are easier to find.
  • Bullet evidence: duties become proof of impact, scope, tools, or outcomes.
  • Relevance: weaker or unrelated details are shortened so they do not bury the match.

Example 1: Marketing coordinator role

The job description asks for campaign coordination, email marketing, social scheduling, reporting, and collaboration with sales.

SectionGeneric versionTailored version
SummaryOrganized marketing professional with experience supporting different business activities.Marketing coordinator with experience supporting email campaigns, social scheduling, campaign reporting, and sales enablement for B2B teams.
SkillsCommunication, organization, teamwork, Microsoft Office, marketing.Email marketing, social scheduling, campaign coordination, CRM updates, performance reporting, sales collateral, stakeholder communication.
Bullet pointHelped the marketing team with campaigns and admin tasks.Coordinated weekly email and social campaign tasks, maintained the campaign calendar, and prepared performance summaries for sales and marketing leads.

The tailored version is stronger because it uses the employer’s language and shows the actual marketing work, not just generic support.

Example 2: Customer success role

The job description asks for onboarding, account retention, customer communication, CRM notes, and product adoption.

SectionGeneric versionTailored version
SummaryCustomer-focused professional with strong communication skills.Customer success associate with experience onboarding users, tracking account health, documenting CRM notes, and supporting retention through proactive follow-up.
SkillsCustomer service, teamwork, problem solving, organization.Customer onboarding, account follow-up, CRM documentation, retention support, product adoption, support handoffs, customer communication.
Bullet pointAnswered customer questions and helped solve problems.Supported onboarding for new customers, documented follow-up actions in the CRM, and escalated product issues to improve adoption and retention.

The tailored version does not exaggerate. It simply connects the candidate’s real support experience to the customer success requirements.

Example 3: NHS nurse role

The NHS job description asks for NMC registration, medication safety, safeguarding, documentation, patient care, MDT communication, and escalation.

SectionGeneric versionTailored version
SummaryHardworking nurse with experience caring for patients in a busy environment.NMC-registered nurse with ward-based experience in patient assessment, medication safety, safeguarding escalation, clinical documentation, and MDT communication.
SkillsPatient care, communication, teamwork, time management.NMC registration, medication administration, patient assessment, safeguarding, NEWS2 escalation, care planning, clinical documentation, MDT handover.
Bullet pointLooked after patients and completed nursing tasks on the ward.Delivered ward-based patient care, completed clinical documentation, supported medication rounds, and escalated changes in patient condition according to local safeguarding and safety procedures.

For NHS applications, tailoring matters because the person specification often names the exact clinical, safety, and communication evidence the recruiter wants to see.

Before-and-after summary formula

A generic summary usually says who you are. A tailored summary says who you are for this role.

Formula partWhat to writeExample
Role targetName the role, specialty, or function you are applying for.Operations coordinator, NHS staff nurse, junior doctor, customer success associate.
Relevant evidenceMention the experience that matches the job description.Scheduling, patient care, audit/QI, onboarding, reporting, stakeholder coordination.
Proof or contextAdd setting, tools, scope, or outcome where possible.Ward-based, B2B SaaS, high-volume clinic, cross-functional team, CRM-based workflow.

Before-and-after skills section

The skills section should not be a random list. It should help the recruiter and ATS connect your CV to the job description.

  • Move the most relevant skills to the top.
  • Use the same standard wording as the job description when it is accurate.
  • Remove broad filler skills if they crowd out role-specific skills.
  • Group related skills when the page is becoming hard to scan.
  • Keep only skills you can support with experience elsewhere in the CV.

Before-and-after bullet points

Generic bullets usually describe tasks. Tailored bullets connect tasks to the requirements of the role.

Weak bulletBetter tailored bullet
Responsible for reports and meetings.Prepared weekly performance reports and coordinated follow-up meetings with operations and sales teams.
Worked with patients and staff.Supported patient assessments, completed accurate care documentation, and communicated changes to the MDT during ward handovers.
Helped customers use the product.Guided new users through onboarding steps, tracked product adoption blockers, and escalated recurring issues to the support team.

What not to change

A tailored CV should still be truthful and recognizable as your career history. Do not add skills you do not have, invent metrics, or copy entire paragraphs from the job advert.

  • Do not claim experience that is not in your background.
  • Do not repeat the same keyword unnaturally.
  • Do not remove important qualifications just because they are not in the advert.
  • Do not make the CV so narrow that your broader value disappears.
  • Do not use a complex design if it makes the tailored content harder to read.

Generic CV vs tailored CV checklist

  • Does the summary mention the type of role you are applying for?
  • Are the top skills aligned with the job description?
  • Do your strongest bullet points prove the must-have requirements?
  • Have you used relevant keywords naturally?
  • Have you removed or shortened details that distract from the target role?
  • Can a recruiter understand your fit within a quick scan?
  • Is the format still simple enough for ATS parsing?
